Types of Chemical Reactions

Unraveling the distinct patterns in how substances transform.

Chemical reactions are fundamental to everything around us, from cooking and digestion to industrial processes. To better understand and predict how chemicals interact, scientists classify reactions into several main types. Let's explore the fascinating ways atoms rearrange themselves!

1. Combination Reaction (Synthesis)

Imagine two or more simple ingredients coming together to form a brand new, more complex dish. That's a combination reaction! In chemistry, two or more reactants (elements or compounds) combine to form a single, larger product. These reactions typically release energy (exothermic).

General Form: A + B → AB

  • Formation of Water: The explosive reaction between hydrogen gas and oxygen gas to produce water. 2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(l)
  • Burning of Magnesium Ribbon: Magnesium metal reacts vigorously with oxygen in the air, producing a dazzling white light and forming magnesium oxide. 2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s)
  • Slaking of Quicklime: Calcium oxide (quicklime) reacts with water to form calcium hydroxide (slaked lime), releasing significant heat. CaO(s) + H2O(l) → Ca(OH)2(aq)

2. Decomposition Reaction

The opposite of combination, a decomposition reaction is like breaking a complex structure down into its simpler building blocks. Here, a single compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances. These reactions always require an input of energy (heat, light, or electricity) to occur, making them typically endothermic.

General Form: AB → A + B

a) Thermal Decomposition

These reactions use .heat energy. to break down a compound. Think of baking or cooking – heat helps break down complex ingredients!

  • Decomposition of Calcium Carbonate (Limestone): This is a major industrial process used in cement and lime production. Limestone is heated strongly to produce quicklime and carbon dioxide gas. CaCO3(s) Heat→ CaO(s) + CO2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Ferrous Sulphate: Green ferrous sulphate crystals (FeSO₄·7H₂O) decompose on heating, losing their water of crystallization and then breaking down further to form reddish-brown ferric oxide, along with sulphur dioxide and sulphur trioxide gases (which have a characteristic smell of burning sulphur). 2FeSO4(s) Heat→ Fe2O3(s) + SO2(g)↑ + SO3(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Lead Nitrate: Heating solid lead(II) nitrate produces yellow lead(II) oxide, reddish-brown nitrogen dioxide gas, and oxygen gas. This is a common lab demonstration. 2Pb(NO3)2(s) Heat→ 2PbO(s) + 4NO2(g)↑ + O2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Potassium Chlorate: Used in the production of oxygen in labs. When heated, potassium chlorate decomposes into potassium chloride and oxygen gas. 2KClO3(s) Heat→ 2KCl(s) + 3O2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Zinc Carbonate: Zinc carbonate decomposes upon heating to form zinc oxide and carbon dioxide. ZnCO3(s) Heat→ ZnO(s) + CO2(g)↑

b) Electrolytic Decomposition (Electrolysis)

These reactions use .electrical energy. to break down a compound, usually in its molten state or as an aqueous solution. This process is vital for extracting highly reactive metals and producing gases like hydrogen and oxygen.

  • Electrolysis of Water: Passing electric current through acidified water breaks it down into hydrogen gas (at the cathode) and oxygen gas (at the anode). This is a clean way to produce hydrogen fuel. 2H2O(l) Electricity→ 2H2(g)↑ + O2(g)↑
  • Electrolysis of Molten Sodium Chloride: Molten (liquid) sodium chloride decomposes into sodium metal and chlorine gas when electricity is passed through it. This is how pure sodium and chlorine are produced industrially. 2NaCl(l) Electricity→ 2Na(s) + Cl2(g)↑
  • Electrolysis of Molten Aluminium Oxide (Hall-Héroult process): This is the primary industrial method for producing aluminum metal from bauxite ore. 2Al2O3(l) Electricity→ 4Al(s) + 3O2(g)↑
  • Electrolysis of Hydrochloric Acid: Dilute HCl solution decomposes into hydrogen gas and chlorine gas upon electrolysis. 2HCl(aq) Electricity→ H2(g)↑ + Cl2(g)↑
  • Electrolysis of Copper Chloride Solution: Copper chloride solution decomposes into copper metal and chlorine gas. CuCl2(aq) Electricity→ Cu(s) + Cl2(g)↑

c) Photolytic Decomposition (Photolysis)

These reactions use .light energy. (specifically sunlight or UV light) to break down a compound. Think of how sunlight can fade colors over time, or how old photographs used light-sensitive chemicals!

  • Decomposition of Silver Chloride: Silver chloride, a light-sensitive compound, decomposes into silver metal and chlorine gas when exposed to sunlight. This reaction is fundamental to black and white photography. 2AgCl(s) Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Cl2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Silver Bromide: Similar to silver chloride, silver bromide also decomposes in the presence of sunlight to form silver metal and bromine gas. This is also used in photography. 2AgBr(s) Sunlight→ 2Ag(s) + Br2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Hydrogen Peroxide: Hydrogen peroxide slowly decomposes into water and oxygen gas when exposed to light, which is why it's usually stored in dark bottles. 2H2O2(aq) Light→ 2H2O(l) + O2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Methane (in atmosphere): In the upper atmosphere, methane can undergo photolytic decomposition initiated by UV radiation. CH4(g) UV Light→ C(s) + 2H2(g)↑
  • Decomposition of Nitrogen Dioxide: Nitrogen dioxide can photolytically decompose into nitrogen monoxide and oxygen when exposed to sunlight. This is a process in air pollution. 2NO2(g) Sunlight→ 2NO(g) + O2(g)↑

3. Displacement Reaction (Single Displacement)

Think of this as one element "kicking out" another element from its compound. In a displacement reaction, a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound. This often occurs when a metal displaces another metal from a salt solution, or a non-metal displaces another non-metal.

General Form: A + BC → AC + B

  • Iron Displacing Copper: When an iron nail is immersed in a blue copper sulphate solution, the iron displaces copper, forming green ferrous sulphate solution and reddish-brown copper metal deposits. Fe(s) + CuSO4(aq) → FeSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
  • Zinc Displacing Hydrogen: Zinc metal reacts with dilute hydrochloric acid, producing hydrogen gas and zinc chloride. Zn(s) + 2HCl(aq) → ZnCl2(aq) + H2(g)↑
  • Chlorine Displacing Iodine: Chlorine gas, being more reactive than iodine, displaces iodine from potassium iodide solution. Cl2(g) + 2KI(aq) → 2KCl(aq) + I2(s)

Displacement Reactions

A displacement reaction is a chemical reaction in which a more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound.

The reactivity series helps us predict which element will displace another.

Key points about Reactivity Series:

  • Elements at the top are more reactive.
  • Elements at the bottom are less reactive.

4. Double Displacement Reaction

This is like two dance partners swapping! In a double displacement reaction, the positive ions (cations) and negative ions (anions) of two reacting compounds simply exchange places to form two entirely new compounds. These reactions often result in the formation of an insoluble precipitate, a gas, or water (neutralization reactions).

General Form: AB + CD → AD + CB

  • Formation of Barium Sulphate Precipitate: When barium chloride solution is mixed with sodium sulphate solution, a white precipitate of barium sulphate is formed along with sodium chloride. BaCl2(aq) + Na2SO4(aq) → BaSO4(s)↓ + 2NaCl(aq)
  • Formation of Silver Chloride Precipitate: Mixing silver nitrate solution with sodium chloride solution immediately forms a white precipitate of silver chloride. AgNO3(aq) + NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s)↓ + NaNO3(aq)
  • Neutralization Reaction (Acid-Base): A classic example where an acid reacts with a base to form a salt and water. This is a type of double displacement. HCl(aq) + NaOH(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H2O(l)
  • Formation of Iron(III) Hydroxide Precipitate: Ferric chloride reacts with ammonium hydroxide to form a reddish-brown precipitate of iron(III) hydroxide. FeCl3(aq) + 3NH4OH(aq) → Fe(OH)3(s)↓ + 3NH4Cl(aq)

5. Oxidation and Reduction Reactions (Redox Reactions)

These reactions are two sides of the same coin, always happening together. Oxidation is the process where a substance .gains oxygen. or .loses hydrogen., or more fundamentally, .loses electrons.. Reduction is the process where a substance .loses oxygen. or .gains hydrogen., or fundamentally, .gains electrons.. They are always paired!

  • Burning of Carbon (Combustion): Carbon reacts with oxygen to form carbon dioxide. Here, carbon is oxidized (gains oxygen). Oxygen is reduced (gains electrons, conceptually, as it binds with carbon). C(s) + O2(g) → CO2(g)
  • Reaction of Zinc Oxide with Carbon: When heated, zinc oxide loses oxygen to form zinc metal (reduced), while carbon gains oxygen to form carbon monoxide (oxidized). Carbon acts as a reducing agent. ZnO(s) + C(s) Heat→ Zn(s) + CO(g)
  • Oxidation of Hydrogen Sulphide by Chlorine: Hydrogen sulphide loses hydrogen (oxidized), while chlorine gains hydrogen (reduced). H2S(g) + Cl2(g) → 2HCl(g) + S(s)
  • Combustion of Methane: Methane burns in oxygen to produce carbon dioxide and water. Methane is oxidized, and oxygen is reduced. CH4(g) + 2O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(l)

Important Notes on Chemical Equations:

  • States of Matter: (s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = aqueous solution (dissolved in water).
  • Reaction Conditions: Symbols like Heat or Electricity or Sunlight written above the arrow indicate specific conditions required for the reaction.
  • Gas Evolution: ↑ (up arrow) indicates a gas being evolved.
  • Precipitate Formation: ↓ (down arrow) indicates an insoluble solid (precipitate) being formed.
